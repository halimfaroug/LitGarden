Module 2: Basic Literary Terms

Learn

Basic literary terms essential for understanding literature.

Moving Beyond the Literal: Figure of Speech - A word or phrase used in a non-literal sense for rhetorical or vivid effect. Non-literal - Not understanding or intending a word or phrase in its original, basic meaning. Rhetorical - The art of effective or persuasive speaking or writing.

The Difference: It is raining. The heavens opened. Why It Matters: Rhetoric transforms basic communication into an art form, allowing writers to persuade, evoke emotion, and create lasting impact.

The Art of Comparison: Simile - Compares two things using “like” or “as” to help visualise something by linking it to the familiar. Her smile was as bright as the sun. Metaphor - A direct comparison suggesting one thing is another without using “like” or “as.” Time is a thief. Analogy - A comparison showing how two things are similar to explain an unfamiliar concept. Just as a sword is the weapon of a warrior, a pen is the weapon of a writer. Why It Matters: Comparison bridges the gap between the writer's imagination and the reader's understanding, making complex or abstract ideas graspable and vivid.

Bringing the World to Life: Imagery (The Senses) - Detailed descriptions appealing to the five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell). The crisp smell of autumn leaves filled the air as the golden sun set behind the trees. Personification (The Life) - Giving human qualities to non-human things or abstract ideas. The wind whispered through the trees. Onomatopoeia (The Sound) - Words that sound like the noise they represent. The clock ticked loudly. Why It Matters: Immersion creates a strong emotional connection. By engaging the senses and animating the inanimate, the story moves from the page into the reader's physical experience.

Module 4: Poetry and Major Aspects of a Poem

Learn

Definition: Composition of verse or comparable patterned arrangement of language in which the expression of feelings and ideas is given intensity by the use of distinctive style and rhythm. Traditionally associated with explicit formal departure from the patterns of ordinary speech or prose, e.g., in the use of elevated diction, figurative language, and syntactical reorder. — Oxford English Dictionary

Core Aspects: It is hard to produce one inclusive definition, but all definitions include the following four aspects: 1. Different patterned arrangement of language (reorder of structure). 2. Use of rhythm. 3. Evoke emotions/feelings. 4. Concentrated and intense.

Poetry Major Elements: 1. Voice 2. Word choice, word order 3. Imagery 4. Figures of speech 5. Sound 6. Form 7. Theme

Structure: A text in poetry is called a poem. A poem is divided into stanzas. Stanzas consists of lines. e.g. "Richard Cory" is divided into four stanzas. Each stanza consists of four lines. (Poem text: Whenever Richard Cory went down town...)

Module 8: Fiction and Major Aspects of Fiction

Learn

Introduction to Fiction. Definition of Fiction: A work invented by the writer, with characters and events that are imaginary.

Printing Press: By Gutenberg in 1440s, Germany. Increase in printing lead to available and affordable books. Mass communication & knowledge circulation. Printing Revolution. Increase in literacy broke monopoly of literate elite: birth of the middle class.

Genres of Fiction by Length: Literature can be classified into different genres by either contextual factors (like historical or cultural contexts), or by textual features (the features and characteristics of the text like setting, characters, style and plot). One of the textual features is length. This is one of the ways that the sub genres of fiction (novel, novella, short story, and short short story) are classified; according to their length.

Fiction by Length: Length is measured by word-count. Novel: extended fictional prose narrative, more than 40,000 words. (the longest of all genres). E.g., Crime and Punishment, Great Expectations ...etc. Novella: Shorter than a novel but still long enough to explore characters deeply and create complicated plot, 10,000-40,000 words. E.g., Animal Farm. Short story: Shorter than the previous two genres, 1000-10,000 words, and it can be read in one sitting.
